Before You Practise — How Interviews Actually Work

Tap any card to understand what interviewers are really scoring. Most candidates fail not because they lack skills — but because they don't understand the game.

🧠 What interviewers actually score
Interviewers assess four things in every answer: clarity (do you understand the question?), structure (can you communicate logically?), evidence (can you prove it, not just claim it?), and self-awareness (do you know your own strengths and gaps?). Technical ability is the entry ticket — the other four decide who gets hired.
📋 How a tech interview is structured
Most tech interviews follow this sequence: (1) recruiter screen — motivation, availability, salary; (2) technical round — coding, systems, or take-home task; (3) behavioural round — STAR-method questions about real situations; (4) final / culture fit — meeting the team lead. Prepare differently for each stage, not with one generic prep.
🤝 Soft skills that decide close calls
When two candidates are technically equal, these win the role: communication (clear, concise, no jargon unless asked), curiosity (genuine questions about the role and team), composure (staying calm when you don't know the answer), coachability (showing you act on feedback), and commercial awareness (understanding that code serves a business outcome).
💡 The STAR method explained
Situation — Set the scene briefly (1–2 sentences). Task — What was your specific responsibility? Action — What did YOU do? (Use "I", not "we".) Result — What was the measurable outcome? Always finish with a number, a percentage, or a clear business impact. Interviewers remember results — they forget descriptions.
🚫 The 5 interview killers
(1) Criticising a previous employer — never, even if it's true. (2) Not having questions prepared — signals low interest. (3) Giving vague answers without examples — "I'm a good communicator" means nothing without a story. (4) Over-explaining when a short answer is better — read the room. (5) Failing to research the company — basic and unforgivable.
🔍 How to research a company properly
Before any interview: check their LinkedIn for recent news and team growth, read their last 3 blog posts or press releases, look at their job posts to understand their stack, find the interviewer on LinkedIn and note their background, and prepare one question that shows you've done this homework. Five minutes of research separates curious candidates from forgettable ones.
